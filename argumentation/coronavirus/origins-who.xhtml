Origins of the SARS-CoV-2 virus

These are arguments abstracted from the WHO-convened Global Study of the Origins of SARS-CoV-2: China Part, published 30 March 2021:

Introduction through a laboratory incident

Laboratory accidents do happen and laboratory infection could happen, so introduction through a laboratory incident is at least possible.

The Wuhan CDC laboratory moved on 2nd December 2019, which might have led to a laboratory incident. However, there were no incidents caused by the lab move.

The fact that the CoV RaTG13 strain was sequenced at the Wuhan Institute of Virology suggests the possibility of introduction through a laboratory incident, but the closest relatives of SARS-CoV-2 are evolutionarily distant from it.

The high quality biosafety at laboratories, the limited number of samples processed, and absence of any record of viruses closely related to SARS-CoV-2 in any laboratory each contradict the hypothesis of Introduction through a laboratory incident.

Introduction through the cold/food chain

Virus persists on packaging; Humans exposed to contaminated fomites; Live virus on packaging; and Screening of packaging suggest the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain. However, Virus not widely circulating.

Animal Cov enteric tropism; Foodborne outbreaks with enteric viruses are common; Seafood is known as a source of foodborne outbreaks; and Oral route could lead to infection suggest the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

Humans shed SARS-CoV-2 through faeces; Sewage contamination; Sewage treatment does not remove all viruses; and Evidence for SARS-CoV-2 nucleic acid in sewage suggest the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

Outbreaks related to imported frozen products suggests the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain; but Virus not widely circulating.

Sewage testing in Spain and Italy suggests the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

SARS-CoV-specific antibodies in food animal handlers suggests the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

No animal infection contradicts the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

Low concentrations on cold-chain products and Food production hygiene suggest Low risk of foodborne transmission.

Low risk of foodborne transmission and No conclusive evidence for foodborne transmission contradict the hypothesis of Introduction through the cold/food chain.

Direct transmission

The arguments in favour of direct zoonotic transmission are:

  • The majority of emerging diseases originate from animal reservoirs and there is strong evidence that most of the current human coronaviruses have originated from animals.

  • Seeding of SARS-CoV-2 in mink populations has shown that these animals are highly susceptible as well and the current evidence available cannot rule out the possibility for minks as the primary source of SARS-CoV-2.

  • Bats are a known reservoir for many zoonotic viruses (with high virus diversity globally); they have the highest proportion of projected zoonotic viruses of any mammalian order. In addition, bat ecology favours virus circulation (large populations, birthing waves, and closely spaced communities).

Also, SARSr-CoV strains have been found in pangolins and various bats; and these do bind to hACE2. However, because several contact residues between the bat and pangolin viruses and the hACE2 receptor are distinct from those in SARS-CoV-2, the affinity is low, and the viruses are genetically still quite distinct from SARSâ€“CoV-2.

An argument against direct zoonotic transmission is the fact that both these putative hosts are infrequently in contact with humans, and an intermediary step involving an amplifying host has been observed for several other emerging viruses (Henipaviruses, influenzaviruses, SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV).

Introduction through intermediate host followed by direct zoonotic transmission

The arguments in favour of transmission via an intermediate host are:

  • Both bat and pangolin putative hosts are infrequently in contact with humans, and an intermediary step involving an amplifying host has been observed for several other emerging viruses (Henipaviruses, influenzaviruses, SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV). SARS-CoV-2 infection and intraspecies spread (including further transmission to humans) has been documented in an increasing number of animal species, particularly mustelids and felids.

  • SARS-CoV-2 adapts relatively rapidly in susceptible animals (such as mink).

  • The increasing number of animals shown to be susceptible to SARS-CoV-2 includes animals that are farmed in sufficient densities to allow potential for enzootic circulation.

  • In high-density farms, there often are connections between farms (for instance, through the workforce and food supply), leading to complex transmission pathways that may be difficult to unravel, as was observed in other zoonotic outbreaks involving farmed animals.

  • Optimized conditions for sustained virus transmission chains in large-scale animal farms may also impact on virus seasonality in favour of a year-round endemic transmission pattern, and thereby increasing the zoonotic risk in winter months.

  • High-density farming is common in many places across the world and includes many livestock species as well as farmed wildlife; and there was a large network of domesticated wild animal farms, supplying farmed wildlife to Wuhan.

The arguments against are:

  • SARS-CoV-2 has been identified in an increasing number of animal species, but genetic and epidemiological studies have suggested that these were infections introduced from humans, rather than enzootic virus circulation.

  • Since the containment of SARS-CoV-2 in China, new outbreaks have occurred for which genomic sequence data was generated. Based on epidemiological analysis and genetic sequencing of viruses from new cases throughout 2020, there is no evidence of repeated introduction of early SARS-CoV-2 strains of potential animal origins into humans in China.

  • There was no genetic or serological evidence for SARS-CoV-2 in a wide range of domestic and wild animals tested to date. The screening of the major livestock species was done across the country and provided no evidence for circulation of a related virus. The scale of testing in these species was such that widespread circulation is extremely unlikely.

  • Screening of farmed wildlife was limited but did not provide conclusive evidence for the existence of circulation.
