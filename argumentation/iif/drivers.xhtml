Change drivers

These are the change drivers listed in the appendix of Intelligent Infrastructure Futures The Scenarios – Towards 2055:

  1. Growing demand for mobility – passengers and goods
  2. Growing skills shortage as infrastructure acquires the skills
  3. Increasing migration (and emigration)
  4. More frequent clashes of multicultural values – faith vs. secular, for example
  5. Growing awareness of the importance of ‘employee liveability’
  6. Increasing importance of the knowledge economy
  7. Ageing, yet more active, population
  8. People face increasing time-intensity
  9. Growth in ‘cyberfraud’
  10. Emergence of better physical and virtual management systems
  11. Satellite location devices
  12. Smart antennas
  13. Increasing use of ‘telepresence’ technology
  14. Converging revolutions in biotech, nanotech, infotech and cognitive science
  15. Culture of control
  16. ‘Real time’ everywhere
  17. Growing debate on housing density in inner cities
  18. Growth of Asian economies
  19. Growing global energy deficit – increased demand and consumption
  20. Emergence of radical solutions to climate change
  21. Declining trust in institutions
  22. Growing crisis in higher education puts the science base under threat
  23. Decline in power of national governments
  24. Increasing world trade
  25. Emergence of networked organisations, clusters and supply chains
  26. New decision-making frameworks
  27. Proliferation of choice
  28. The rise of pan-regional hubs
  29. The end of affluence
  30. Increasing emphasis on sustainable design
  31. Rise of ‘zero waste’ movement
  32. Changing patterns of demand for housing in some areas
  33. E-commerce continues to grow
  34. Increasing focus on tourism and its contribution to climate change
  35. Decoupling of tourism and transport
  36. Rising tension between freedom of information and privacy
  37. Emergence of megacities
  38. Changing family and household structures
  39. The rise of ‘slow’
  40. Growing utilisation of ‘embedded’ technology
  41. Continued growth of an ‘always on’ culture
  42. Semi-autonomous/autonomous vehicles becoming safer and more efficient
  43. Grids and networks create shared capacity
  44. ‘Digital natives’ – growing up accustomed to technology
  45. Reducing cost of ICT and enhanced data processing
  46. High-speed rail travel
  47. Growing gap between rich and poor
  48. Continued capital underinvestment
  49. Growing (impact of) climate change
  50. Increasingly localised/decentralised energy production
  51. Relatively low spend on energy research and development
  52. Increasing consumer desire for social and environmental responsibility and transparency
  53. Growth of the surveillance society
  54. Complex just-in-time models are vulnerable to external shock
  55. Demand management of transport provision
  56. Changing data storage: from desktop to network
  57. Movement away from office-based working
  58. Emerging debate around provision of ‘citizen’s income’
  59. Rising importance of local provision
  60. Taxation increasingly based on resource consumption rather than income
  61. Move towards full-cost accounting
  62. Emerging infrastructure, emerging cultural form
